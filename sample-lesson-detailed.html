Sample Lesson

Here's what a real session with Coach Liam looks like. Every minute has a purpose.

No two players are the same, so no two sessions are exactly alike. But every lesson follows the same philosophy: build skills, build confidence, build a smarter player. Here's a look at what a typical 40-minute session looks like.

0–7 min

Warm-Up & Ball Comfort

We start loose. The goal here is to get your kid comfortable with the ball in their hands — not running laps.

  • Ball slaps, wraps around the waist, figure-8s through the legs
  • Dribble in place — right hand, left hand, alternating
  • "Simon Says" with the basketball — makes it a game, keeps attention

Coach's note: A lot of young players grip the ball too tight or dribble looking straight down. This warm-up fixes that naturally without me having to lecture about it.

7–17 min

Dribbling Foundations

This is where we build the base. Everything in basketball starts with being able to handle the ball.

  • Stationary crossovers — slow, controlled, both directions
  • Walking dribble down the court — right hand there, left hand back
  • "Protect the ball" drill — I try to knock it away, they learn to shield
  • Cone weave at walking pace — focus on control, not speed

Coach's note: I never tell a beginner to go fast. Speed comes from confidence, and confidence comes from not losing the ball. We build from the ground up.

17–27 min

Shooting Basics

Form first. Always. We're not chucking threes — we're building a shot that'll last.

  • BEEF basics — Balance, Eyes, Elbow, Follow-through
  • Close-range form shooting — 3-5 feet from the basket
  • Layup walkthrough — footwork first (left-right-up for the right side)
  • "Make 5" from each spot before moving back

Coach's note: Parents sometimes ask why we're shooting so close. Because a bad habit from far away is harder to fix than a good habit from up close. Trust the process.

27–35 min

Game Situation: 1v1 Lite

Now we put it all together — but low pressure. This is where the fun really kicks in.

  • 1-on-1 from the elbow — I play passive defense at first
  • Read and react — "Can you get past me? Or should you pull up?"
  • Celebrate good decisions, not just made baskets
  • Quick "what would you do?" scenarios between possessions

Coach's note: This is my favorite part. You can see the lightbulb go off when a kid makes a read instead of just driving into traffic. That's growth.

35–40 min

Cool Down & Homework

We finish with free throws and a quick talk about what we worked on.

  • 10 free throws — count the makes, track progress week to week
  • One thing they did great today (positive reinforcement)
  • One thing to practice at home — always simple, always doable
  • "Driveway challenge" — something fun they can do on their own

Coach's note: I want every kid to leave feeling like they got better. Because they did. And the homework isn't busy work — it's the stuff that makes next session even better.

0–7 min

Dynamic Warm-Up & Handle Work

No standing around. We get moving immediately with combo dribble sequences.

  • Full-court dribble series — crossover, between-the-legs, behind-the-back
  • Two-ball dribbling (stationary, then walking)
  • Reaction drills — I call out moves, they execute on the fly
  • Speed ladder footwork with ball in hand

Coach's note: By intermediate, the warm-up IS the workout. These aren't easy drills — they're the same combos you'll see varsity guards use. We just slow them down enough to build real muscle memory.

7–17 min

Attack Moves & Finishing

Now we're building an offensive toolkit — specific moves for specific situations.

  • Jab step series — shot fake, drive left, drive right, pull-up
  • Pick-and-roll reads — when to turn the corner, when to pull back
  • Finishing through contact — off-hand layups, reverse finishes
  • Mid-range game — elbow jumpers, baseline pull-ups, floaters

Coach's note: I teach every player at least three ways to score from the same spot. If you only have one move, the defense only needs one answer. We make them a problem to guard.

17–24 min

Defensive Intensity

This is where we separate. Anyone can score in practice. Defense takes discipline.

  • Closeout technique — sprint to the ball, chop your feet, low stance
  • Slide drills with live ball handler — stay in front, don't reach
  • Help-and-recover rotations — read the pass, move early
  • 1-on-1 defensive possessions — three stops to get off the court

Coach's note: Defense isn't about being the most athletic. It's about effort and positioning. I've seen smaller players lock down bigger ones because they understood angles. That's what we build here.

24–33 min

Live Play: Read & React

Full-speed 1-on-1 and situational play. This is where everything we've worked on gets tested.

  • 1-on-1 full court — offense has to score, defense has to get a stop
  • Advantage/disadvantage scenarios — start with the defense behind
  • "Decision drills" — two options on every play, must choose in 2 seconds
  • End-of-game situations — down 2, ball in your hands, 10 seconds left

Coach's note: I put players in uncomfortable spots on purpose. Not to stress them out — to show them they can handle it. Confidence doesn't come from easy reps. It comes from doing hard things and succeeding.

33–40 min

Shooting & Film Review

We cool down with shooting and talk through what happened.

  • Spot shooting — 5 spots, 3 makes from each before rotating
  • Free throws under fatigue — simulate late-game pressure
  • Quick film review on phone — "watch this play, what did you see?"
  • Set goals for the week — specific, measurable, not generic

Coach's note: The film review is only 2-3 minutes, but it's huge. When a player can watch themselves and identify what they did right or wrong — that's when real improvement starts happening on its own.

Every session is tailored to your player.

These are sample structures — the real lesson adapts to what your kid needs most. Beginners who are ahead move faster. Intermediate players who need fundamentals get them. No cookie-cutter workouts.
